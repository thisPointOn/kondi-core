# Real Estate Decision Framework: Buy vs. Rent Analysis Tool

## Section 1: Core Decision Framework

Making the buy-vs-rent decision requires evaluating three critical factors in sequence. This framework helps you assess whether homeownership makes financial sense for your specific situation.

### Decision Tree: Three Sequential Tests

**START HERE → Test 1: Price-to-Rent Ratio**

First, calculate your local price-to-rent ratio:
```
Price-to-Rent Ratio = Median Home Price ÷ (Monthly Rent × 12)
```

For example: $400,000 home price ÷ ($1,800/month × 12) = 18.5 ratio

**Interpretation:**
- **Ratio > 25**: Strongly favor renting. You're paying a significant premium for ownership versus the cost of renting.
- **Ratio < 18**: Favor buying. Home prices are relatively affordable compared to rent.
- **Ratio 18-25**: Gray zone. Other factors become decisive.

**Why this matters:** This ratio captures the fundamental economics of your local market. High ratios mean you're paying a premium for ownership over renting flexibility.

**↓ If ratio is 18-25, OR if you want to validate a buying decision → Test 2**

**Test 2: Location Confidence**

Ask yourself: "How confident am I that I'll stay in this metro area for 5+ years?"

**Threshold:** You need >70% confidence to seriously consider buying.

**Why 5 years matters:** Transaction costs (realtor fees, closing costs, moving) typically total 8-10% of home value. You need 4-5 years of appreciation and principal paydown to break even. Earlier moves risk losing money.

**Calculate your confidence level by considering:**
- Job stability and career trajectory in this location
- Family ties and social network depth
- Partner's career flexibility
- Industry concentration (can you find another job locally if needed?)

**↓ If confidence > 70% → Test 3**

**Test 3: Financial Buffer**

After making your down payment and covering closing costs, do you have 6+ months of expenses in emergency savings?

**Formula for true affordability:**
```
Required Liquid Assets = Down Payment + Closing Costs (3-5% of price) + (6 × Monthly Expenses)
```

**Why this matters:** Homeownership brings unexpected costs that renting doesn't:
- Major repairs (roof, HVAC, foundation): $5,000-$25,000+
- Property tax increases
- Job loss while locked into a mortgage
- Market downturns that prevent selling

Without adequate reserves, a single major expense can trigger a financial crisis. A 6-month buffer provides genuine security.

**DECISION OUTCOMES:**

✅ **Consider Buying If:** Ratio favorable (<20) OR marginal (18-25) AND location confidence >70% AND 6-month buffer exists

❌ **Strongly Consider Renting If:** Ratio >25 OR location confidence <70% OR insufficient emergency fund

⚠️ **Seek Professional Advice If:** Results are borderline or your situation involves complexity (self-employment, stock compensation, family planning)

## Section 3: Conditional Decision Heuristics

Use these IF-THEN-BECAUSE statements to apply the framework to your specific circumstances:

**IF** your local price-to-rent ratio is >28 AND you're uncertain about 5-year location plans, **THEN** strongly lean toward renting **BECAUSE** you're paying a large premium for flexibility you may need, and early sale transaction costs will compound losses.

**IF** your ratio is <18 AND you have >70% location confidence AND a solid emergency fund, **THEN** seriously consider buying **BECAUSE** the economics favor ownership and you have stability to weather market fluctuations.

**IF** your ratio is 20-24 (middle range) AND you're starting a family or planning to within 2 years, **THEN** weight stability highly **BECAUSE** moving with young children is disruptive, and school district continuity becomes valuable even if finances are marginal.

**IF** you're in a high-growth career phase (expecting 30%+ income growth over 5 years), **THEN** consider how renting preserves geographic flexibility for career opportunities **BECAUSE** a better job in another city might deliver far more value than home equity, and you don't want housing to constrain your career trajectory.

**IF** your ratio is borderline (18-22) AND your market shows rising inventory (90+ days), **THEN** you may have time to wait for better prices or rates **BECAUSE** elevated inventory signals buyer leverage, but only if you're not sacrificing significant rent increases by waiting.

**IF** you're tempted to "time the market" by waiting for a crash, **THEN** proceed with extreme caution **BECAUSE** market timing is notoriously difficult, you may wait years while paying rising rent, and mortgage rates often rise when prices fall (reducing affordability gains).

**IF** your location confidence is marginal (60-70% range), **THEN** assign significant value to renting flexibility **BECAUSE** the financial cost of selling early exceeds most people's intuition, and you're essentially buying expensive optionality you may not use.

**IF** you're comparing a ratio of 22 in a constrained market (San Francisco) versus 22 in an oversupplied market (Phoenix), **THEN** weight supply conditions heavily **BECAUSE** constrained markets have higher appreciation potential but also higher risk if you need to sell during a downturn, while oversupplied markets may see continued price pressure.

## Section 4: Risks & Next Steps

### Risks You Accept Either Way

**Buying Risks:**
- **Illiquidity:** Cannot quickly access your equity without selling or borrowing
- **Maintenance costs:** Unpredictable major repairs (roof, foundation, HVAC)
- **Market risk:** Home prices can decline, leaving you underwater on your mortgage
- **Opportunity cost:** Down payment capital could generate returns elsewhere

**Renting Risks:**
- **Rent increases:** Annual increases typically 3-8%, sometimes much higher in hot markets
- **No equity building:** Monthly payments don't build ownership stake
- **Displacement risk:** Landlord can sell or decide not to renew lease
- **Lifestyle constraints:** Limited ability to customize or renovate your space

### How to Apply This Framework

1. **Calculate your local ratio:** Find median home price and rent for your target neighborhood (not metro-wide averages). Use Zillow, Redfin, or Apartments.com.

2. **Assess location confidence honestly:** Don't confuse "I like living here" with "I'll stay 5+ years." Consider job security, career trajectory, and family factors.

3. **Run complete financial numbers:** Calculate total monthly costs (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, HOA, 1-2% maintenance). Calculate: `Total Monthly Cost ÷ Comparable Rent = Ownership Premium`.

4. **Seek professional advice if:** Your ratio is 18-22 (borderline), you have complex finances, or you're considering major life changes.

### When This Framework Doesn't Apply

This framework assumes typical circumstances. It may not apply if you have:
- Significant wealth (housing costs negligible relative to income)
- Non-financial priorities (school district, multigenerational living, accessibility)
- Investment strategies (house-hacking, rental property plans)
- Extreme market conditions (vacation destination, company town)
- Family assistance (gifted down payment)

Use the framework as a baseline, but weight your unique circumstances appropriately.
