# Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

## General Questions

### 1. Why not just do hybrid? Why force a choice between Path A and Path B?

**Short answer**: "Hybrid" is vague and often devolves into the worst of both worlds—remote workers feel like second-class citizens, and in-office workers don't get the collaboration benefits.

**Longer answer**: Hybrid typically means "everyone works some days remote, some days in office." This creates several problems:

- **Unclear defaults**: When should we meet in person vs. remotely? Who decides?
- **Split investment**: You pay for both office space AND remote infrastructure, optimizing for neither
- **Proximity bias risk**: Remote days exist, but those who choose more office days get advantages
- **Meeting dysfunction**: Some people in room, some remote—consistently poor experience

This framework forces clarity: Either you're optimizing for remote-first (Path A) with intentional in-person, or you're optimizing for in-office (Path B) with remote flexibility. Both can work. Vague hybrid usually doesn't.

**Exception**: If you're large enough to allow *team-level* decisions (Product team chooses Path A, Sales team chooses Path B), that can work—but it requires careful coordination at team boundaries.

---

### 2. What if we choose the wrong path?

**You can change.** This framework includes 12-month success metrics specifically so you can evaluate whether your chosen path is working.

**If Path A isn't working** (remote-first is failing):
- Pivot to Path B: Add office requirement with anchor days
- Intermediate step: Require more in-person gatherings before full pivot

**If Path B isn't working** (in-office requirement causing problems):
- Pivot to Path A: Remove office requirement, build async infrastructure
- Intermediate step: Reduce required office days (4→3→2)

**Key principle**: Policy should serve your organization, not the reverse. If data shows it's not working, change it.

**Warning**: Don't change reactively based on loud voices or quarterly whims. Use the success metrics to make data-driven decisions after giving the policy time to work (minimum 6 months, ideally 12 months).

---

### 3. Can individual teams make their own decisions about remote vs. in-office?

**It depends on your size and structure.**

**If you're <100 people**: Company-wide policy is usually clearer and more equitable. Team-level decisions create confusion.

**If you're 100-500 people**: Team-level decisions *might* work if:
- Teams are truly independent (minimal cross-team collaboration)
- You're willing to invest in both remote AND in-office infrastructure
- You have clear rules about cross-team meetings and equity

**If you're >500 people**: Team-level decisions are more viable but require:
- Clear guidelines (teams can't just "wing it")
- Equity monitoring across teams
- Shared infrastructure for both paths
- Executive alignment that both paths are acceptable

**Watch out for**: Teams choosing based on manager preference rather than capability, proximity bias if some teams are remote and others aren't, resentment between teams.

---

### 4. We scored "Build Capability Before Deciding." How long will this take?

**Typically 3-6 months**, depending on your gaps.

**If your gaps are**:
- **Documentation/Communication (Categories 1-2)**: 3-6 months to build basic async infrastructure
  - **Focus**: Document 20 critical processes, train on async communication, establish meeting norms
- **Onboarding/Performance Management (Categories 3,5)**: 4-6 months to build systems
  - **Focus**: Create structured onboarding program, define performance metrics, train managers
- **Strategic Priorities (Category 6)**: Requires leadership alignment, could be 1 month or 6 months depending on level of disagreement
  - **Focus**: Align on talent strategy, office real estate plans, executive commitment

**Action plan**:
1. Identify your 3 lowest-scoring categories
2. Assign owners to each category to build capability
3. Set 90-day checkpoint to reassess progress
4. Retake assessment at 3 or 6 months
5. Don't rush to policy decision until capability is built—it will fail otherwise

**Why this matters**: Choosing Path A without documentation capability leads to chaos. Choosing Path B without bias monitoring leads to inequity. Build the foundation first.

---

### 5. What about international teams? Does this framework work across countries?

**Yes, with modifications.**

**Path A (Remote-First)** generally works better for international teams because:
- You're already dealing with timezone spread (async is required)
- Less feasible to require in-office for employees in different countries
- Documentation and async norms are essential

**Path B (In-Office)** can work internationally if:
- Each country has its own office and in-office requirement
- Anchor days are set per office (not globally)
- Cross-country meetings are scheduled with timezone equity
- You accept that international collaboration will be async

**Special considerations**:
- **Legal/tax**: Ensure you have legal entities in each country you hire
- **Timezone spread**: If >6 hours, Path A is almost always better
- **Cultural norms**: Some countries have stronger office culture; adjust expectations accordingly
- **Equipment/stipends**: Adjust amounts for cost of living and local norms

**Watch out for**: Headquarters-centric thinking (scheduling everything for HQ convenience), assuming US norms apply globally.

## Policy-Specific Questions

### 6. How do we handle exceptions and accommodations?

**Clear process is essential.** Vague exception processes lead to favoritism and inequity.

**Require**:
1. **Formal request**: Written request through HR or designated system
2. **Documentation**: Medical documentation for disability, written context for life circumstances
3. **Timely review**: Decision within 5-10 business days
4. **Defined duration**: Temporary accommodations have end date and review process
5. **Consistency**: Same criteria applied to all requesters

**Common accommodations**:
- **Disability**: ADA requires reasonable accommodation; default to yes with interactive process
- **Temporary medical**: Own or family member recovery—grant generously with defined end date
- **Caregiving**: Temporary childcare/eldercare disruption—grant with defined period
- **Long commute** (Path B): Case-by-case; may grant reduced office days for >90 min commutes
- **Relocation** (Path B): If employee moves, evaluate whether remote work or separation

**What not to accommodate**:
- ❌ Preference without documented need
- ❌ "I'm more productive at home" without evidence (that's a policy disagreement, not accommodation)
- ❌ Requests that fundamentally undermine the policy (e.g., "I want to be full-time remote" under Path B without legitimate accommodation need)

**Transparency**: Share (anonymized) accommodation stats quarterly to build trust that process is fair.

---

### 7. What if employees refuse to comply with the policy?

**Progressive discipline**, same as any policy violation.

**Path B (In-Office) non-compliance**:
1. **First instance**: Manager conversation—understand if there's a legitimate barrier or accommodation need
2. **Pattern (2-3 instances)**: Formal warning; document in performance file
3. **Continued non-compliance**: Performance improvement plan
4. **Persistent refusal**: Termination (after clear warnings and opportunity to comply)

**Path A (Remote-First) non-compliance** (examples: not documenting decisions, missing 24-hour response SLAs, security violations):
- Same progressive approach
- Focus on coaching first—these may be skill gaps, not willful violations

**Important**:
- Distinguish between *inability to comply* (needs accommodation) and *refusal to comply* (performance issue)
- Apply consistently—don't let high performers skirt the rules
- Be willing to lose employees who won't comply; inconsistent enforcement undermines everyone else's compliance

**Reality check**: If >20% of employees are non-compliant, the problem is the policy (too strict, poorly designed, not explained well), not the employees.

---

### 8. How do we prevent "two-tier" culture where remote workers are disadvantaged?

**This is the critical equity challenge for Path A (remote-first).**

**Prevention mechanisms**:

**1. Monitor and measure**:
- Track promotion/compensation rates by location
- Track performance ratings by location
- Track access to high-visibility projects
- Investigate disparities >10 percentage points

**2. Output-based evaluation**:
- Define clear metrics for each role
- Base evaluations on results, not visibility or responsiveness
- Blind promotion reviews (remove location data)

**3. Structured career development**:
- Formal mentorship programs (don't rely on organic networking)
- Required quarterly career development conversations
- "Brag documents" where employees track accomplishments
- Project showcases for visibility

**4. Inclusive meeting practices**:
- Default to all-remote meetings (everyone on own screen) or all-in-person
- No hybrid meetings where some people are in room and others remote (always disadvantages remote)
- Meeting materials shared in advance; recording posted after

**5. Leadership modeling**:
- Executives must work remotely regularly to experience it
- Senior leaders must be distributed, not all at HQ
- Celebrate remote employees' contributions visibly

**Watch for**: Comments like "I haven't seen Jane much" as proxy for performance, last-minute hallway decisions, promotion packets that emphasize "executive presence."

---

### 9. For Path B (in-office), how do we prevent proximity bias?

**This is the critical equity challenge for Path B (in-office requirement).**

**Prevention mechanisms**:

**1. Monitor and measure**:
- Track promotion/compensation by office attendance (minimum required vs. above minimum)
- Track performance ratings by office attendance
- **Red flag**: If 5-day-in-office employees promoted at higher rates than 3-day employees, you have bias

**2. Explicit rules**:
- Office attendance beyond minimum CANNOT be a promotion factor (train managers on this)
- Important decisions must happen on anchor days when everyone is present
- Using allocated remote days cannot negatively affect reviews

**3. Manager training**:
- Bias awareness training
- Output-based evaluation training
- Calibration sessions to review promotion decisions for bias

**4. Anonymous performance reviews**:
- Remove attendance data from promotion packets during review
- Focus on outcomes delivered, not time seen in office

**5. Cultural enforcement**:
- Call out positive comments about "always being in the office" as inappropriate
- Celebrate results regardless of where they were delivered
- Ensure remote days are fully normalized, not requiring justification

**Watch for**: Comments like "Mike's always here, he's so dedicated," scheduling important discussions on Fridays when people use remote days, rewarding voluntary office attendance.

---

### 10. What about new parents, caregivers, or employees with disabilities?

**These groups often need flexibility.** Both paths must accommodate them, but differently.

**Path A (Remote-First)**:
- Generally more accommodating by default
- BUT: "Remote" ≠ "working while caregiving"—childcare still required during work hours
- Flexibility comes from choosing work hours, not simultaneous caregiving

**Path B (In-Office)**:
- May be harder for caregivers (commute + office time)
- Accommodations: Reduced office days, flexible office hours (come late/leave early), remote work during specific caregiving periods
- Nursing parents: Private spaces in office, flexibility for pumping schedules

**Both paths**:
- **Disability accommodations**: Required under ADA (US) and equivalent laws—grant generously
- **Pregnancy**: May need modified schedules, remote work before/after leave
- **Eldercare**: Temporary accommodations for crisis periods
- **Childcare emergencies**: Sick kid = work from home day

**Key principle**: Flexibility for caregiving is about *when* and *where* work happens, not *whether* work happens or performance standards being lowered.

**Equity watch**: Ensure caregiving accommodations available to all genders, not just mothers.

## Implementation Questions

### 11. How long does implementation take?

**Minimum 6 months from decision to full rollout**, often 12 months.

**Realistic timeline**:
- **Month 1**: Assessment and decision
- **Month 2**: Communication and planning
- **Months 3-5**: Infrastructure build, training, pilot
- **Month 6**: Full launch
- **Months 7-12**: Measurement and iteration

**Why so long?**
- Infrastructure takes time (documentation, office redesign, tools)
- Training needs to be thorough
- Pilots surface issues to fix before full rollout
- Change management requires time for adaptation

**Don't rush**: Launching prematurely without infrastructure leads to failure and expensive reversals.

**Can you move faster?** If you're small (<50 people), have existing infrastructure, or are choosing Path B with minimal changes, potentially 3-4 months. But don't skip training or measurement.

### 12. What does this cost?

**Costs vary by path and current state.**

**Path A (Remote-First) costs**:
- **Home office stipends**: $1,500-2,500/employee one-time + $500-1,000/year ongoing
- **Internet reimbursement**: $75-150/employee/month = $900-1,800/year
- **Co-working stipends** (optional): $200-300/employee/month = $2,400-3,600/year
- **In-person gatherings**: $2,000-5,000/employee/year (flights, hotels, venue)
- **Tools and training**: $10,000-100,000 one-time (knowledge base, training development)
- **Savings**: Office space reduction (potentially $5,000-15,000/employee/year in high-cost cities)

**Path B (In-Office) costs**:
- **Office space**: $5,000-15,000/employee/year (varies wildly by city)
- **Office amenities**: $1,000-3,000/employee/year (cafe, furniture, meeting rooms)
- **Commuter benefits**: $1,000-2,000/employee/year (transit, parking subsidies)
- **Home office** (for remote days): $500-1,000/employee one-time + $50-100/month internet
- **Talent constraint cost**: Harder to measure, but may pay premium for local talent or take longer to hire

**Both paths**:
- **IT infrastructure**: VPN, security, collaboration tools ($50-200/employee/year)
- **Manager/employee training**: $10,000-50,000 one-time
- **HR administration**: Tracking, policy management, accommodations

**ROI calculation**: Compare costs to benefits (access to talent, productivity, employee retention). Many companies find Path A has lower total cost, but Path B may have better collaboration ROI for specific work types.

### 13. What if we can't afford the stipends/benefits in these templates?

**Adjust to your budget.** The templates use reasonable benchmarks, but you can customize.

**Lower-cost options**:
- **Equipment**: Provide core equipment (laptop, one monitor) but not full home office stipend
- **Internet**: Reimburse only if employee needs upgrade for work (not blanket reimbursement)
- **Co-working**: Offer only for employees who can't work from home, not universal stipend
- **In-person gatherings**: Once per year instead of twice, regional gatherings instead of flying everyone

**What NOT to cut**:
- ❌ Basic equipment needed to do the job (laptop, monitor for full-time computer work)
- ❌ Security requirements (VPN, encrypted devices)
- ❌ Training (critical for success)
- ❌ Equity monitoring (essential to prevent discrimination)

**If you truly can't afford remote infrastructure**: Path B may be more cost-effective for you (share office equipment, no home stipends). This is a legitimate factor in path selection.

### 14. How do we change policies mid-year if it's not working?

**Carefully, with transparency and transition time.**

**Process**:
1. **Identify failure**: Use success metrics to document specific failures
2. **Analyze root cause**: Is the policy wrong, or is implementation poor?
3. **Consider fixes short of policy change**: Can you adjust implementation rather than changing the core policy?
4. **If policy change is needed**:
   - Announce with ≥60 days notice (more for major changes)
   - Explain rationale with data
   - Provide transition support
   - Acknowledge impact on employees who planned around old policy

**Examples**:
- **Path A failing**: Add required in-person weeks quarterly as intermediate step before full Path B pivot
- **Path B failing**: Reduce required office days from 4→3 before full Path A pivot
- **Specific equity issues**: Adjust promotion process, manager training, not necessarily whole policy

**What to avoid**:
- ❌ Changing policy reactively based on executive mood
- ❌ Frequent changes (creates whiplash and distrust)
- ❌ Announcing changes with <30 days notice (unless emergency)
- ❌ Changing without explaining why (breeds cynicism)

**Credibility hit**: Every policy change costs credibility. Use the 12-month review process to make considered changes, not constant tweaks.

---

### 15. Can we do a permanent pilot or "experiment"?

**No. Indefinite pilots fail.**

**Why pilots fail**:
- Employees don't truly adapt ("this is temporary, I'll wait it out")
- You don't invest fully ("it's just a pilot, let's not spend money yet")
- Data is muddled (mixing pilot and non-pilot behavior)
- It drags on for years in limbo

**Better approach**:
- **Time-limited pilot**: 6-12 month pilot with defined end date and decision criteria
- **At end of pilot**: Make formal decision to adopt, modify, or abandon based on data
- **Communicate clearly**: "We're piloting for 6 months, then deciding"

**If you're not ready to commit**: That signals you should do more assessment work, not launch a pilot. Pilots work when you're 80% confident and need to validate, not when you're 50% confident.

## Legal and Compliance Questions

### 16. What are the legal risks of each path?

**Both paths have legal considerations.**

**Path A (Remote-First) legal risks**:
- **Geographic compliance**: Hiring in new states/countries creates tax, employment law obligations
- **Workers' comp**: Home office injuries may be covered; ensure appropriate insurance
- **Security/privacy**: Remote work creates data security risks (require VPN, encryption)
- **Wage/hour**: Harder to monitor hours for non-exempt employees (require time tracking)
- **Discrimination**: If certain groups disproportionately struggle with remote, could face claims

**Path B (In-Office) legal risks**:
- **Discrimination**: If office requirement disproportionately impacts protected groups (parents, disabled, certain races/genders), could face claims
- **ADA compliance**: Must provide accommodations for disabilities; office requirement can't be blanket
- **Commute time**: Generally not compensated, but ensure clarity for non-exempt employees
- **Retaliation**: Can't penalize employees for using remote days they're entitled to
- **Safety**: Responsible for safe office environment

**Both paths**:
- **Consistency**: Apply policy consistently; favoritism creates legal risk
- **Documentation**: Document accommodations, exceptions, performance issues
- **Good faith**: Show you're monitoring for equity and addressing disparities

**Recommendation**: Have employment lawyer review your policy before launch.

---

### 17. How does this work with employment laws in different states/countries?

**You must comply with local laws**, which may override or constrain your policy.

**Common variations**:
- **California**: Strict requirements for expense reimbursement (must reimburse remote work costs)
- **New York City**: "Right to disconnect" proposals (limits after-hours communication)
- **European Union**: Strong worker protections, work hour limits, data privacy (GDPR)
- **Brazil, India, others**: Different employment law frameworks

**What this means**:
- **Stipend amounts**: May need to be higher in states requiring full reimbursement
- **Work hours**: May need to enforce limits in jurisdictions with mandatory time off
- **Data handling**: Additional security requirements in GDPR jurisdictions
- **Termination**: Harder to terminate for policy non-compliance in some countries

**Action**: Consult with legal counsel in each jurisdiction where you hire to ensure compliance.

---

### 18. What about union environments or collective bargaining agreements?

**Remote work policy may be subject to collective bargaining.**

**If you have a union**:
- **Review your CBA**: Is work location a mandatory subject of bargaining?
- **Engage union early**: Don't implement without discussing with union
- **Negotiate terms**: Office requirements, equipment, stipends may need to be negotiated
- **Memorandum of understanding**: Formalize remote work terms in MOU

**If you're implementing in union environment**:
- Be prepared for longer timeline (negotiation takes time)
- Union may have equity concerns (ensure monitoring addresses them)
- Equipment and stipends may be negotiating points (budget accordingly)

**Non-union**: Easier to implement unilaterally, but still monitor for equity to prevent organizing risk.

## Success and Measurement Questions

### 19. What if we're meeting some metrics but not others?

**Partial success is common in year 1.** Focus on:

**1. Which metrics are you missing?**
- Missing engagement but hitting attrition → People are staying but not happy (address experience)
- Missing attrition but hitting engagement → People like it but leaving (address compensation/career)
- Missing equity metrics → Critical—address immediately with bias interventions

**2. Are you improving?**
- If trend is positive (getting closer to targets each quarter), give it more time
- If trend is flat or negative, intervene now

**3. Root cause analysis**:
- Is the policy wrong, or is implementation poor?
- Is it a training issue, tools issue, or cultural issue?

**Decision framework**:
- **Meeting 5-6 metrics**: Success, continue
- **Meeting 3-4 metrics**: Partial success, make targeted improvements
- **Meeting 0-2 metrics**: Failure, significant changes or pivot needed

**Example**: If you're Path B and hitting all metrics except hiring time-to-fill, you might:
- Make certain hard-to-fill roles remote-eligible
- Open satellite office in new city
- Increase compensation to compete for local talent
- But not necessarily abandon Path B entirely

---

### 20. How do we benchmark against other companies?

**Benchmarking is tricky because policies vary wildly.**

**Useful benchmarks**:
- **Engagement scores**: Compare to industry/company size benchmarks (e.g., tech company 100-500 employees)
- **Attrition**: Industry benchmarks (e.g., 18% for tech, 10% for finance)
- **Time-to-fill**: Industry and role-specific benchmarks

**Not useful to benchmark**:
- Remote vs. office policy directly (too context-dependent)
- Specific practices (e.g., "Company X requires 4 days, so we should too")

**Where to find benchmarks**:
- **Engagement**: CultureAmp, Lattice, Gallup publish industry benchmarks
- **Attrition**: LinkedIn, Glassdoor, industry associations
- **Compensation**: Radford, Pave, Option Impact (for equity)

**Better than external benchmarks**: Your own baseline (pre-policy) and your own trends over time.

---

### 21. What if employees game the metrics?

**Example**: Employees artificially boost engagement scores to prevent policy change.

**Mitigation**:
1. **Multiple data sources**: Don't rely only on self-reported surveys
   - Combine: Surveys + attrition data + performance data + exit interviews
2. **Qualitative + quantitative**: Numbers + themes from focus groups
3. **Anomaly detection**: If scores spike unnaturally, investigate
4. **Anonymous feedback channels**: Ways to share concerns without fear
5. **Trend over time**: One-time spike is suspicious; sustained improvement is real

**Reality check**: If employees are happy enough to "game" the system to keep the policy, the policy is probably working. The bigger risk is leadership ignoring bad data because they like the policy.

## Edge Cases and Special Situations

### 22. What about interns, contractors, and part-time employees?

**Interns**:
- **Path A**: Can work remotely but strongly recommend in-person for at least 50% of internship (mentorship, learning)
- **Path B**: Subject to same office requirements as full-time, or designate as remote-only if that serves learning goals

**Contractors**:
- Policy typically doesn't apply (they set their own terms)
- If contractor works on-site, they follow office policies (badge, security, etc.)
- Can't require contractors to work from office if contract doesn't specify it

**Part-time employees**:
- **Path A**: Same expectations (async, documentation) on days they work
- **Path B**: Prorated office requirements (e.g., if working 3 days/week, maybe 2 days in office)

**Key**: Be clear in job descriptions and contracts what the work arrangement is.

---

### 23. What about sales, customer-facing, or travel-heavy roles?

**These roles may not fit standard policy.**

**Sales**:
- Often need to be in field with customers
- **Path A**: Default remote, travel to customers, quarterly team gatherings
- **Path B**: May still be mostly on the road, not in office; clarify expectations

**Customer-facing** (retail, support, field service):
- Defined by customer location, not office policy
- May be customer site, remote, or on-call
- **Action**: Clearly mark these roles as "Customer Location-Based" distinct from office-based or remote

**Travel-heavy** (consultants, field engineers):
- Similar to sales—may not be in office or home
- **Path A**: Works well, default remote with client travel
- **Path B**: Clarify whether anchor days apply when not traveling

**Principle**: Tailor policy to role requirements; don't force-fit roles that don't match the model.

---

### 24. How do we handle major life events (moving, sabbatical, parental leave)?

**Moving/Relocation**:
- **Path A**: Generally fine as long as still in country with legal entity; may need timezone accommodation
- **Path B**: If moving beyond commutable distance, evaluate: Transfer to different office, remote exception, or separation

**Sabbatical**:
- Not a remote work question—this is leave
- On return, same policy applies

**Parental leave**:
- **During leave**: Not working, so policy doesn't apply
- **Return from leave**: May need flexible return (part-time, modified schedule)
- **Path B**: Consider temporary reduction in office days for first 4-8 weeks back

**Medical leave**:
- Return to work with accommodations as needed
- May need temporary full remote or reduced hours

**Principle**: Life events may require temporary accommodation, but permanent changes require permanent accommodation process.

---

### 25. What if an executive or key leader refuses to follow the policy?

**This is a leadership crisis, not a policy problem.**

**Why it matters**: Executives who undermine policy destroy it. If CEO says "I don't believe in documentation" on Path A, you don't have a remote-first culture.

**Options**:

**1. Address directly**:
- Executive sponsor (board chair, CEO) has conversation: "You need to model this or we need to change course"
- Make clear that undermining policy damages credibility and employee trust

**2. Carve out exception**:
- "Executives are exempt from this policy"
- **Warning**: This creates two-tier culture and resentment
- Only viable if executives are small % of company and isolated from most employees

**3. Change the policy**:
- If key leaders won't follow it, you chose the wrong path
- Better to acknowledge this and change than to have policy-in-name-only

**Realistic take**: If you chose Path A and your executives won't work asynchronously or document their decisions, Path A will fail. Pivot to Path B. If you chose Path B and executives won't come to office, your employees won't either. Address this before launch, not after.
