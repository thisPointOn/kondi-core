# Remote Work Capability Assessment

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates your organization's current capabilities to execute either a remote-first or in-office policy successfully. Answer each question honestly based on **current state**, not aspirations.

**Time Required**: 60-90 minutes with your leadership team

**Participants**: Include representatives from Leadership, HR/People Ops, Engineering/Product, and at least one individual contributor.

## How to Use This Assessment

1. **Answer all 28 questions** with YES or NO based on your current organizational state
2. **Calculate your score** using the rubric at the end
3. **Receive your recommendation**: Path A (Remote-First), Path B (In-Office), or Build Capability First

**Important**: This is a capability assessment, not a preference survey. Answer based on what your organization can demonstrably do today, not what you wish you could do.

---

## Category 1: Documentation Culture

Documentation culture determines whether knowledge persists beyond individual conversations and office hallways. Remote-first organizations cannot function without it; in-office organizations can compensate with synchronous knowledge transfer.

### Q1: Process Documentation
**Do you maintain up-to-date written documentation for at least 80% of recurring processes (onboarding, deployment, approval workflows, etc.)?**

- YES: We have documentation, it's current (updated in last 6 months), and people actually use it
- NO: Documentation is missing, outdated, or exists but people ask questions instead of reading it

**Why this matters**: Remote-first requires written knowledge. In-office allows "just ask Jamie" solutions. Neither is wrong, but they require different infrastructure.

---

### Q2: Decision Documentation
**Are decisions consistently documented with context, not just conclusions?**

Example: Meeting notes include WHY a decision was made, what alternatives were considered, and what success looks like—not just "We decided to do X."

- YES: 80%+ of decisions are documented this way
- NO: We document what was decided, but rarely the reasoning

**Why this matters**: Remote workers need context to execute independently. In-office workers can ask clarifying questions informally. Both approaches work in the right environment.

---

### Q3: Public-by-Default Information Sharing
**Is your default to make information widely accessible rather than sharing it with specific people?**

Example: Team updates are posted in a shared channel/wiki rather than emailed to specific stakeholders.

- YES: Most information (excluding confidential items) is posted publicly first
- NO: We share information with specific people who we think need it

**Why this matters**: Remote workers can't overhear relevant conversations. Public-by-default creates discoverability. In-office relies on networks and hallway awareness.

---

### Q4: Searchable Communication History
**Can employees search and find answers to common questions in your communication tools?**

- YES: We have searchable archives (Slack/Teams history, wiki, etc.) and people use them before asking
- NO: History exists but isn't organized/searchable, or culture is to ask questions rather than search

**Why this matters**: Remote-first scales through self-service information access. In-office scales through asking people directly.

---

### Q5: Documentation Maintenance
**Do you have assigned owners for key documentation who keep it current?**

- YES: Critical docs have owners, review dates, and are kept current
- NO: Docs are created but not maintained; no clear ownership

**Why this matters**: Stale documentation is worse than no documentation. Remote-first requires sustained documentation discipline.

---

## Category 2: Communication Norms

Communication norms determine whether work can happen asynchronously across time zones or requires real-time presence.

### Q6: Async Communication Default
**Can employees get most work done without requiring same-day responses?**

- YES: Typical response time is 24 hours; work is structured to avoid same-day dependencies
- NO: Work requires rapid back-and-forth; >50% of questions need same-day answers

**Why this matters**: Remote-first across time zones requires async norms. In-office optimizes for synchronous collaboration.

---

### Q7: Meeting Recording and Notes
**Are meetings recorded or have detailed notes that non-attendees can reference?**

- YES: 80%+ of meetings are recorded or have comprehensive notes posted within 24 hours
- NO: We take notes but they're sparse, or we don't record/post them consistently

**Why this matters**: Remote workers in different time zones can't attend all meetings. In-office workers can catch up informally.

---

### Q8: Written Communication Quality
**Do employees write clear, complete messages that minimize back-and-forth?**

Example: Instead of "Can we talk about the project?", employees write "I need to decide X by Friday. Here's my context... What do you think?"

- YES: Most written communication is structured and complete
- NO: Written communication is often vague; requires follow-up questions

**Why this matters**: Remote-first requires precise written communication. In-office can clarify through conversation.

---

### Q9: Meeting Necessity Filtering
**Do you have clear criteria for when a meeting is necessary vs. when async communication suffices?**

- YES: We have guidelines and people follow them; unnecessary meetings are rare
- NO: Default is to schedule a meeting when collaboration is needed

**Why this matters**: Remote-first minimizes synchronous overhead. In-office can afford more meetings since they're less disruptive.

---

### Q10: Cross-Timezone Consideration
**When scheduling meetings, do you actively minimize inconvenience to people in non-headquarters time zones?**

- YES: We rotate meeting times, record sessions, or default to async for distributed teams
- NO: Meetings are scheduled for headquarters convenience; remote workers adjust

**Why this matters**: Remote-first treats all locations equally. In-office centers on physical location.

---

## Category 3: Onboarding Systems

Onboarding systems reveal whether new employees can succeed independently or need physical proximity to learn.

### Q11: Structured Onboarding Program
**Do you have a documented, week-by-week onboarding program for new hires?**

- YES: Every new hire follows the same structured program with defined milestones
- NO: Onboarding is informal; varies by manager or involves lots of "I'll introduce you to people"

**Why this matters**: Remote onboarding requires structure. In-office can rely on osmosis and informal introductions.

---

### Q12: Self-Service Onboarding Content
**Can new hires answer most onboarding questions through documentation rather than asking people?**

- YES: Comprehensive onboarding docs; new hires rarely need to ask where to find information
- NO: New hires need to ask lots of questions; info is in people's heads

**Why this matters**: Remote hires can't tap someone on the shoulder. Self-service is essential.

---

### Q13: Remote Onboarding Track Record
**If you've hired remote employees in the past 2 years, did they reach productivity at the same rate as in-office hires?**

- YES: Remote hires reach full productivity in the same timeframe as office hires
- NO: Remote hires take longer to ramp, or we haven't hired remotely

**Why this matters**: Past performance predicts future success. If remote onboarding already works, remote-first is viable.

---

### Q14: Buddy/Mentor Assignment
**Do you assign dedicated buddies/mentors to new hires with clear responsibilities?**

- YES: Every new hire gets a buddy; the role is defined with specific check-in cadence
- NO: Buddy assignment is informal or doesn't happen

**Why this matters**: Both paths benefit from this, but it's critical for remote where chance encounters don't happen.

---

## Category 4: Bias Mitigation

Bias mitigation determines whether remote or in-office workers face systematic disadvantages in career progression.

### Q15: Proximity Bias Awareness
**Does your organization actively monitor for proximity bias (in-office employees receiving more opportunities)?**

- YES: We track promotion/raise rates by location; actively investigate disparities
- NO: We assume fairness or haven't specifically tracked by office vs. remote

**Why this matters**: In-office policies create proximity bias risk. Monitoring is essential for Path B. Path A has different bias risks (timezone, communication style).

---

### Q16: Promotion Criteria Documentation
**Are promotion/advancement criteria explicitly documented and applied consistently?**

- YES: Written criteria exist; promotion decisions reference specific evidence against criteria
- NO: Promotions are based on manager judgment; criteria are informal

**Why this matters**: Both paths need this, but remote-first can't rely on visibility and "executive presence" as proxies for performance.

---

### Q17: Performance Evaluation Based on Output
**Are performance evaluations based on measurable output rather than observed activity?**

- YES: Evaluations focus on results delivered, not hours seen in office or responsiveness
- NO: Being visible, available, or "working hard" influences evaluations

**Why this matters**: Remote-first requires output-based evaluation. In-office can fall into "butts-in-seats" metrics.

---

### Q18: Inclusive Meeting Practices
**When some participants are remote, do you enforce practices that level the playing field?**

Examples: Everyone joins on their own screen (no conference room camera), chat questions are answered verbally, materials shared in advance.

- YES: We have explicit practices and enforce them
- NO: Hybrid meetings favor in-room participants

**Why this matters**: Path B (in-office requirement) still has remote days; inclusive practices are essential. Path A makes this easier by defaulting to all-remote.

---

## Category 5: Performance Management

Performance management capabilities determine whether you can evaluate and develop employees without physical oversight.

### Q19: Clear Performance Metrics
**Do individual contributors have clear, measurable performance expectations?**

- YES: Every IC role has 3-5 specific metrics or deliverables they're accountable for
- NO: Performance expectations are general ("do good work", "be a team player")

**Why this matters**: Remote-first requires clarity. In-office can rely on observation and informal feedback.

---

### Q20: Regular 1-on-1s
**Do managers conduct weekly or biweekly 1-on-1s with direct reports?**

- YES: 90%+ of managers do this consistently with documented notes
- NO: 1-on-1s are irregular or don't happen

**Why this matters**: Both paths need this, but it's critical for remote where informal check-ins don't happen organically.

---

### Q21: Feedback Culture
**Do employees regularly give and receive specific, actionable feedback?**

- YES: Feedback is normalized; happens in real-time and in formal reviews
- NO: Feedback mostly happens in annual reviews or when problems escalate

**Why this matters**: Remote workers need explicit feedback; they can't read body language or sense dissatisfaction.

---

### Q22: Manager Training
**Do managers receive training on managing remote/distributed teams?**

- YES: We provide training and ongoing support for distributed management
- NO: We haven't trained managers specifically on remote management

**Why this matters**: Managing remote requires different skills. Assuming in-office management skills transfer is a common failure mode.

---

## Category 6: Strategic Priorities

Strategic priorities determine whether remote-first or in-office better serves your business objectives.

### Q23: Geographic Talent Access
**Is access to talent in non-headquarters locations a strategic priority?**

Examples: You're hiring for hard-to-fill roles, want to reduce compensation costs, or need 24/7 coverage.

- YES: Geographic expansion is a strategic priority in the next 2 years
- NO: We prefer to hire within commuting distance of existing offices

**Why this matters**: Remote-first enables geographic expansion. In-office constrains to commutable radius.

---

### Q24: Office Real Estate Commitment
**Do you have long-term office leases (3+ years) that represent significant fixed costs?**

- YES: We have multi-year leases and would lose substantial money by going remote-first
- NO: Our leases are short-term or we're willing to absorb the cost

**Why this matters**: Economic reality matters. Long-term leases don't preclude remote-first but affect the timeline and cost.

---

### Q25: Collaboration Intensity
**Does your core work require high-frequency, synchronous collaboration?**

Examples: Early-stage startups doing rapid iteration, creative agencies with constant brainstorming, incident response teams.

- YES: Our work requires multiple real-time collaboration sessions per day
- NO: Most work is heads-down with periodic collaboration touchpoints

**Why this matters**: High-sync work is easier in-office but can be done remotely with good tools and processes. This question assesses current collaboration intensity.

---

### Q26: Security/Compliance Requirements
**Do you have security or compliance requirements that are significantly easier to meet in a controlled office environment?**

Examples: HIPAA, financial services regulations, classified work.

- YES: Remote work creates substantial compliance complexity or risk
- NO: We can meet our compliance requirements remotely with standard security practices

**Why this matters**: Some industries have legitimate compliance constraints that favor in-office. Don't choose remote-first if it creates unmanageable legal risk.

---

### Q27: Executive Commitment to Remote-First
**If choosing Path A (Remote-First), do executives genuinely support it?**

This means: Will executives give up reserved parking, corner offices, and proximity to power? Will they model async communication and documentation?

- YES: Executives are committed to remote-first norms even if inconvenient for them
- NO: Executives prefer in-office or would be unwilling to change their work habits

**Why this matters**: Remote-first fails when executives undermine it. Honest assessment prevents expensive false starts.

---

### Q28: Investment Willingness
**Is your organization willing to invest 6-12 months and material budget in building capabilities?**

Examples: Manager training, documentation infrastructure, bias monitoring systems, equipment stipends.

- YES: We'll commit budget and time to build missing capabilities
- NO: We want to decide and implement quickly with minimal new investment

**Why this matters**: Path A requires investment in async infrastructure. Path B requires office space and equity monitoring. Neither is free.

#### Path A: Remote-First Recommended

Choose Path A if you meet **ALL** of the following:
- ✅ Total score ≥ 20 (71%+)
- ✅ Category 1 (Documentation) ≥ 4/5
- ✅ Category 2 (Communication) ≥ 4/5
- ✅ Q27 (Executive Commitment) = YES
- ✅ Q28 (Investment Willingness) = YES

**Rationale**: You have or can build the capabilities needed for remote-first success. Your strategic priorities align with geographic distribution.

**Next Step**: Proceed to [Remote-First Policy Template](./policy-template-path-a-remote-first.md)

---

#### Path B: In-Office Requirement Recommended

Choose Path B if:
- ✅ Total score < 20, OR
- ✅ Category 1 (Documentation) ≤ 2/5, OR
- ✅ Category 2 (Communication) ≤ 2/5, OR
- ✅ Q27 (Executive Commitment) = NO, OR
- ✅ Q26 (Security/Compliance) = YES

**Rationale**: Your organization functions better with synchronous collaboration and physical presence. This is a valid choice that plays to your strengths.

**Next Step**: Proceed to [In-Office Policy Template](./policy-template-path-b-in-office.md)

---

#### Build Capability Before Deciding

If you don't clearly fit Path A or Path B:
- ⚠️ Total score 15-19 AND mixed category scores
- ⚠️ Q28 (Investment Willingness) = NO but aspiring to Path A
- ⚠️ Leadership team disagrees significantly on answers

**Rationale**: You have capability gaps that will undermine either policy. Address these first.

**Next Step**: 
1. Identify your 3 lowest-scoring categories
2. Spend 3-6 months building capability in those areas
3. Retake this assessment
4. **Do not** implement a new policy until you have clear results

**Focus Areas**:
- If Documentation or Communication scored low: Invest in documentation infrastructure and async communication training
- If Bias Mitigation or Performance Management scored low: Build explicit evaluation systems and train managers
- If Strategic Priorities scored low: Align leadership on talent strategy and business objectives

## Interpreting Edge Cases

### "We scored high but executives aren't committed (Q27 = NO)"
→ **Choose Path B.** Remote-first without executive buy-in fails expensively. Be honest about this constraint.

### "We scored low but want to be remote-first"
→ **Build capabilities first.** Wanting remote-first doesn't make it viable. Invest 6 months in documentation and async communication, then reassess.

### "We have high security/compliance needs (Q26 = YES)"
→ **Path B is safer unless** you have dedicated InfoSec resources to build remote-compliant systems. Don't create legal risk for policy preferences.

### "Our team is split on many questions"
→ **Don't proceed yet.** Split answers indicate lack of shared understanding. Do a 2-week observation period: track how work actually happens, then reassess with data.

## What This Assessment Does NOT Measure

This assessment evaluates capability, not preference. It doesn't measure:
- ❌ Employee preference for remote vs. office (that's a survey, not a capability assessment)
- ❌ Real estate costs (financial analysis is separate)
- ❌ Productivity differences (requires controlled measurement)

Those factors matter, but they're inputs to policy customization, not the path decision.
